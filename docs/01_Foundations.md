1. Foundations of Intelligence
The Modular, Unified Mind
Modern neuroscience suggests that human intelligence is not a single, monolithic entity
. Instead, the brain is modular yet unified
. It contains specialized subsystems—such as the visual cortex for seeing, the motor cortex for movement, and the hippocampus for memory—while simultaneously acting as a coordinating system that produces a single sense of "self"
. Both unity and specialization coexist
. This biological tension is very similar to the architectural differences between Dense and Mixture of Experts (MoE) AI models
.
The Layered Intelligence Stack
Intelligence exists at multiple scales, where each layer learns from the previous one
:
Cell intelligence: Cells make local decisions
.
Organ intelligence: Organs coordinate biological functions
.
Brain intelligence: The brain integrates signals
.
Social intelligence: Social groups create shared intelligence
.
Civilizational intelligence: Culture stores memory across generations
.
Distributed and Embodied Intelligence
A crucial, yet rarely discussed insight is that intelligence is not located purely inside an individual
. It emerges from the interaction of the body, environment, society, history, and evolution
. This concept is scientifically known as Distributed or Embodied Intelligence
. Human learning relies heavily on these interactions through observation (mirror neurons), social transmission (language and teaching), embodied interaction (touching and failing physically), and the reshaping of neural pathways over time
.
Biology vs. AI
Currently, humans possess a fundamentally richer system than AI
. Humans experience bodily sensations, rely on emotions as decision systems, face survival pressure, and possess long-term evolutionary memory and social identity
. AI, on the other hand, relies primarily on statistical prediction, lacks intrinsic motivation, and currently possesses no real physical embodiment
.
From a biological perspective, life itself is a long-running learning algorithm where DNA acts as compressed survival memory and evolution is a slow learning process across millions of years
.
